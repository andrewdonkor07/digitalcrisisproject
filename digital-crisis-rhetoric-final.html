Abstract

This digital humanities project examines how the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) and the American Red Cross (ARC) communicated on X (formerly Twitter) and Instagram during the California wildfires from 2020 to 2025. Building on a dataset of 143 posts across both platforms, the project uses qualitative rhetorical analysis, dialogic coding, and DH visualization to surface how organizational identity and platform affordances together shape the possibilities of crisis communication.

"How do organizational type and platform logics shape the rhetorical and dialogic possibilities of crisis communication during extended natural disasters — and what does this mean for publics' capacity to participate in meaning-making?"

A consistent pattern emerged: FEMA relied on logos-driven, procedural rhetoric across both platforms — eligibility criteria, deadlines, application links. ARC used pathos-driven narrative — survivor stories, emotional appeals, community identity. These reflect distinct institutional identities that are amplified, constrained, and transformed differently by X's text-threading affordances and Instagram's visual grammar. The project names these modes digital bureaucracy and digital humanitarianism.

Complicating this analysis is a deeper methodological question that is, as Dr. Moro notes, "upstream" of much of social media studies. The dramatic restriction of API access since 2018 — what Bruns (2019) calls the APIcalypse — has foreclosed the kind of large-scale automated social media research that was common in the 2010s. This project's manually curated dataset is not a workaround but a theoretical position: an argument that close reading, practiced in the DH tradition (Walsh, 2023), is a principled methodology for studying closed-platform discourse. This reframes the smallness of the dataset as an argument about platform power rather than a research limitation.

Historical Context

Communications Infrastructures Accrete

Following Starosielski (2015), this project situates the 2020–2025 case within two temporal arcs: the deep history of disaster communications infrastructure, and the specific historical conditions of the internet between 2020 and 2025.

Pre-Digital Era · 1900s–1980s
Radio, Wire Services, and the Birth of Official Disaster Communication
FEMA-equivalent agencies relied on radio broadcasts and wire service press releases for mass public communication during disasters. The one-to-many broadcast model — institutional authority → passive public — was structurally embedded in the medium. Interactivity was impossible by design (Quarantelli, 1988; Mileti, 1999). This is the deep template that institutional disaster communication still partially follows.
Early Internet Era · 1990s–2000s
Static Web and Email: A One-Way Web, Slightly Wider
The rise of organizational websites and email listservs expanded institutional reach without fundamentally altering the broadcast model. FEMA's 2001 post-9/11 web presence and early Red Cross donation pages mark this transition. Infrastructure (Starosielski, 2015) — servers, bandwidth, domain registrations — accretes historically along the same institutional tracks as radio and wire networks.
Open Social Media Era · 2009–2018
Twitter, Open APIs, and the First Wave of Social Crisis Communication
Twitter's open API (launched 2006, matured ~2009) enabled scholars to collect large datasets and study crisis communication at scale. This period produced the SMCC model (Austin et al., 2012, 2017) and most of the empirical base for crisis communication on social media. Crises like Hurricane Sandy (2012) and the Boston Marathon bombing (2013) became scholarly case studies. Organizations like FEMA and ARC built social media communication teams. The "open" era of social media research flourished — and lasted barely a decade.
COVID-19 Interregnum · 2020–2022
Pandemic Temporality Meets Wildfire Temporality on the Same Feeds
The 2020–2025 wildfire years cannot be understood apart from COVID-19. As Dr. Moro's feedback notes, lockdowns accelerated internet use and transformed how publics conceptualized disaster. The slow-moving, constitutive temporality of the pandemic — omnipresent, chronic, invisible — intermingled on social media with the localized burst temporality of wildfires: sudden, visible, geographic. FEMA and ARC navigated this double-disaster context simultaneously. Social media platforms, particularly X and Instagram, became primary channels of disaster information for the first time for many users.
Platform Transformation · 2022–2025
Elon Musk, API Closures, and the End of the Open Social Web
Elon Musk's acquisition of Twitter (October 2022) and its rebranding as X dramatically altered the platform's structure, content moderation, verification systems, and — crucially for research — API access. The 2023 API pricing changes made automated data collection economically and practically impossible for most academic researchers (Freelon, 2018; Bruns, 2019). Instagram's simultaneous tightening of its own API access meant that by 2023–2025, the "social media of 2020" was structurally different from the "social media of 2025." The posts in this dataset were produced in radically different platform environments depending on their year.
Present Condition · Post-APIcalypse
Manual Curation as Disciplinary Response
This project's dataset was assembled through deliberate manual curation of publicly available posts. This is not a limitation — it is a methodological contribution. Following Bruns (2019), Freelon (2018), and Walsh (2023), the closure of social media APIs is itself the primary object of study: platform black-boxing (Chun, 2011; Gillespie, 2014) as it affects an entire discipline's analytical capacity. Close reading of a small, carefully selected corpus is the DH tradition (Drucker, 2012) and the appropriate response to the post-API condition.

Post-API Methods
Deen Freelon

Coined "the post-API age" to describe the epochal shift in social media research access. Argues this requires disciplinary rethinking, not workarounds — exactly the "conceptual step back" Dr. Moro advocates.
Post-API Methods
Lomborg & Bechmann
Journal of Information Technology & Politics (2014)
Using APIs in social media research: methodological considerations and ethical questions. Among the first to theorize both the affordances and the limits of API-based social media research.
Crisis Communication
W. Timothy Coombs
Corporate Reputation Review (2007)
Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT) distinguishes instructing information (safety) from adjusting information (emotional support). FEMA's posts map clearly to instructing; ARC's to adjusting.
Crisis Communication
Austin, Jin & Liu
SMCC Model (2012, 2017)
Social-Mediated Crisis Communication: publics draw simultaneously on official channels, news, and peer networks. Trust and authenticity matter as much as content. Crises are multivocal — a point this project's analysis of replies bears out.
Crisis Communication
danah boyd
A Networked Self (2010)
Networked publics are not passive recipients. They search, share, quote-tweet, and interpret institutional messages through their own social positions — a finding visible in the divergent reply patterns in this dataset.
Rhetorical Theory
Bitzer, Vatz & Burke
Philosophy & Rhetoric (1968, 1973); Rhetoric of Motives (1969)
Classical rhetoric attends to exigence, who constructs it, and how identification operates across institutional and public discourse. The foundational vocabulary for this project's analysis of FEMA's and ARC's rhetorical situations.
Rhetorical Theory
Kent & Taylor
Public Relations Review (2002)
Dialogic theory of public relations: genuine organizational communication requires mutuality, propinquity, empathy, risk, and commitment. The five-principle framework used in this project's dialogic coding.
Methodology

How This Research Was Done

A multi-method approach combining qualitative rhetorical analysis, dialogic coding, platform affordance analysis, and DH visualization — grounded in a deliberate stance on the post-API condition.

Qualitative Textual Analysis
Each post coded for rhetorical appeals (ethos, pathos, logos), tone, subject focus, call-to-action type, and emotional register. Two coding passes per post, cross-checked for consistency.
Dialogic Feature Coding
Posts evaluated against Kent & Taylor's (2002) five dialogic principles: mutuality, propinquity, empathy, risk, and commitment. Each marked present or absent per post.
Platform Affordance Analysis
Attention to X-specific elements (threading, quote-tweets, character limits, link previews) vs. Instagram-specific elements (carousels, stories, Reels, visual grammar, caption-link constraints).
DH Visualization (Chart.js)
Visualizations built with Chart.js following Drucker's (2009, 2012) speculative computing and D'Ignazio & Klein's (2020) feminist data ethics — treating every chart as a rhetorical argument, not a neutral display.
Networked Publics Sample
A limited sample of publicly visible replies and quote-tweets analyzed to show how networked publics reframed and extended (or contested) institutional messages (Boyd, 2010).
Cross-Platform Comparative
Paired analysis comparing how each organization's rhetorical profile shifts between X and Instagram — revealing that platform affordances amplify existing organizational tendencies rather than transforming them.

The APIcalypse as Upstream Problem — and This Project's Response

Freelon (2018) named the structural shift as the "post-API age." Bruns (2019) documented the APIcalypse's consequences for Twitter research specifically. By 2023–2025, both X and Instagram had made automated data collection economically and technically impossible for most academic researchers. This means the field of social media crisis communication faces an "upstream" problem: the very methods that produced its empirical base (large-scale API-pulled datasets) are no longer available.

This project's manually curated dataset is a deliberate methodological response. Rather than treating this constraint as a flaw requiring apology, the project theorizes manual curation — aligned with DH close reading traditions (Drucker, 2012; Walsh, 2023) and feminist data ethics (D'Ignazio & Klein, 2020) — as a principled, reproducible methodology for post-API social media scholarship. The smallness of the corpus is a finding about platform power, not a research limitation. Future expansion to Palladio-based geographic mapping and D3.js network visualization would extend this approach.
